Deeper Dive into Beal Conjecture
The Beal Conjecture remains a cornerstone of modern number theory, extending Fermat’s Last Theorem (FLT) to unequal exponents. To recap and expand: No coprime positive integers A, B, C exist with x, y, z > 2 satisfying A^x + B^y = C^z. Solutions with common factors are allowed, but primitive (coprime) ones are forbidden.
Deeper mathematical insights: The conjecture’s proof landscape involves arithmetic geometry. For instance, Darmon-Granville (1995) showed that for fixed x, y, z, only finitely many primitive solutions exist via Faltings’ theorem on finite rational points on curves. Masser (1998) linked it to the abc conjecture, proving that if abc holds with radical rad(abc) and quality q >1, then Beal follows for sufficiently large exponents (since A^x ≈ C^z implies rad(A B C) ≈ C, forcing q<1 contradiction for large z). Shinichi Mochizuki’s inter-universal Teichmüller theory (IUT, controversially accepted in some circles by 2025) implies abc, thus Beal for high exponents, but full resolution awaits universal acceptance.
Computational verification: No counterexamples up to exponents ~10^6 via distributed searches (e.g., Beal Prize efforts). Partial proofs include: Siksek (2009) for x=3, y=3, z>3; Fermat-Catalan cases (x=y=3, z=3 solved by Mihăilescu 2002). Challenges stem from exponential growth: Brute force impossible beyond small bounds, requiring modular or descent methods.

Deeper Dive into Inverse Galois Problem

Deeper Dive into Inscribed Square Problem
Toeplitz’s conjecture: Every Jordan curve admits an inscribed square. Solved for convex/smooth (Schnirelmann, Stromquist), open for pathological curves (e.g., Osgood monsters).
Deeper insights: Approaches use equivariant topology (e.g., SO(2) actions on configuration spaces) or measure theory (almost-everywhere squares via ergodicity). Challenges: Wild curves may embed Cantor sets without aligned points. Partial: Inscribed rectangles (Meyerson), rhombi exist; squares require diagonals/angles. Modern: Computational verification for polygons, persistent homology for topological invariants.
